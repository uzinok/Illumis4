When PMS symptoms become unbearable - this is premenstrual dysphoric disorder (PMDD), which affects 7-8% of women. Dr Louise Hayes, an obstetrician-gynaecologist with more than 20 years of experience, will explain what it is and how to manage it.

Last updated by Lydia Smith Peer reviewed by Dr Sarah Jarvis MBE
Last updated 20 Jun 2018
6 mins read

- Hi, can you tell me the difference between PMS and PMDD?

- Hello. Women with premenstrual dysphoric disorder experience a more severe form of PMS. I have personally known several women who developed depression due to PMDD, as well as the development of serious gynaecological diseases, including the development of malignant tumours and infertility.

- That's terrible. And how do you know when you're not just PMSing, but dysphoric disorder?

- These are usually standard PMS symptoms, but there are differences. PMDD is diagnosed if there are at least 5 indicators:

  • feelings of anxiety, sadness, stress;
  • uncontrollable anger and depression;
  • suicidal thoughts;
  • panic attacks, uncontrollable tearfulness;
  • mood swings, anger;
  • loss of interest in daily activities;
  • feelings of chronic fatigue and a sense of loss of control;
  • sleep disturbances and loss of attention control;
  • Gastrointestinal disorders: abdominal bloating, overeating;
  • headaches and joint pains.

- Tell me, what is the reason for the development of a more serious course of premenstrual syndrome?

- There is no exact cause. Scientists believe that it is hormonal changes that occur throughout the menstrual cycle. There is also a predisposition in women with cancer and mental illness. Women who may be more sensitive to changes in serotonin levels may be more likely to experience PMDD.

- What is the treatment when diagnosed with this condition?

- Modern approaches to the treatment of PMDD consist of multifaceted therapy, both with medication and normalisation of diet, physical activity and psychological factors. Medications can be sedative and hormonal, depending on the situation and severity of symptoms. But as a rule, it is a large list of drugs that may not be combined with each other.

- So hormones and painkillers don't completely take away the symptoms?

- Unfortunately this is true. When treating physical symptoms, the pain may not go away completely. With psychological problems, mood swings cannot be completely controlled. Hormones contribute to lower metabolism and endocrinological problems. Of course some people can't give them up for medical reasons, but there are those who don't need to.

I am often approached by female athletes who are not allowed to take a large list of drugs, as they have a negative effect on diet, metabolism, endurance and can show up as doping in analyses.
